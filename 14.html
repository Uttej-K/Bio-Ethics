14: Should in-vitro fertilization be an option for a woman?

The arguments against IVF treatments are primarily of two kinds : religious and medical.

The Catholic church has been a very vocal opposition to IVF treatments. The arguments are quite similar to those against abortion - concerns regarding whether a zygote should be treated as a person with their own rights and whether, the discarded zygotes during IVF treatment equate to degrading the respect for human dignity.

These arguments are rooted less in reality and more in archaic ideas that are steadily loosing relevance in today's times. Family structures and gender roles are changing and ideas and rules that were developed centuries ago predominately by men should not have to dictate what happens with women's bodies even today.

It is hard to be persuaded by these 'judgements' rather than arguments from the Church or any religious group. Further, 'wasted' zygotes are merely a consequence of conception - whether that was through IVF or natural methods. There is also nothing particularly alarming about the case of taking stem cells from zygotes as this does not seem to kill the zygote itself.

However, IVF does have a few medically concerning issues that need to be evaluated.

The most important of these is that the long terms effects of IVF are rarely understood by the women choosing to undergo such treatments. There is also a growing concern that IVF treatments could lead to increased chances of cancers. Since this is a technology that has come into usage much more time and resources need to be dedicated to the long terms effects of the powerful hormones used to produce ovum for IVF.

There is also a high chance of infection through out this procedure due to many zygote insertions and ovum removals from the women's womb. However, this can be avoided to a large extent by ensuring good, uncompromised quality of care in infertility treatment centers. For this, information regarding the rates of success and the medical history of the clinic need to be easily accessible to the customers. Further, measures such as limiting the number of cycles of treatment could also help prevent customers being charged a lot of money or place unfair burden on their bodies for treatments with low chances of success.

Further, may patients are rarely fully aware of the possible negative outcomes and are misled regarding its success rates. Ectopic pregnancies, sudden abortions and miscarriages are much more frequent in IVF and could be fatal to the mother. There is also a higher chance of birth defects, especially in cases of multiple pregnancies.

Since, the rate of success is variable, many implant multiple zygotes for a better chance of conception. However this could result in multiple pregnancies which the mother might be unprepared for. Raising in an additional child or two puts high demands on mental, physical and financial resources of the family. Further, due to their moral beliefs against abortion many choose to keep all the babies. This can be avoided by making Single embryo transfer (SET) the norm as it has nearly the same rates of success.

If IVF treatments seem to place such huge demands from the mother why is it still so popular even when there are alternatives available?

The societal norm dictates that motherhood is what completes a woman. Even in face of changing societal and gender roles, the idea of motherhood is heralded as the essence of being a woman. It is precisely this unseen pressure that pushes many women to go through tedious procedures to fulfil that ideal. The idea of a childless women is still perceived with negative connotations speaks to the heteronormative bias to our perceptions of womanhood. It is time that society comes to terms with the possibility that women could choose to remain childless.

Additionally, there is an undue emphasis on genetic relationship with the child. While there is certainly some influence of genes on the child's life, much of the child's personality depends on the circumstances in which one is raised. However, due to preconceived notion regarding continuing one's genetic lineage, many do not even consider adoption - a far less burdening and an equally rewarding process.

However, in face of these issues banning IVF is not the logical conclusion. Every medical treatment poses risks that are willingly taken. Chemotherapy could harm many organs and endanger the very life it wants to save. But banning chemotherapy would leave people who need it to consider other, perhaps more unreliable options.

The approach towards IVF and infertility at large, should be multipronged. The role of health, diet and exercise must be emphasized while also providing multiple options including IVF.
