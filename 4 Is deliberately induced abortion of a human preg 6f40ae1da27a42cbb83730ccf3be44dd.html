4: Is deliberately induced abortion of a human pregnancy ethically justifiable?

Abortion, has been a deeply divisive topic in current global politics. While the world struggles to move towards a utopian future where the liberties and rights of each of its citizens are upheld, it raises the inevitable question of who is exempt from being included in this list.

The arguments regarding abortion seem to primarily contend with the question of should the unborn fetus and the mother be given equal say in this matter? These lead to the more far-branching questions such as what constitutes a human being? Should vegetative patients or infants be considered any different from the fetus in terms of how alive they are?

The responses to this question seem to span a range of opinions with the extreme right arguing that abortion of any kind should be banned and the extreme left upholding the belief that abortion does not require a justification.

Theses extremes of the argument seem to come primarily from fear and a threat to personal values rather than a careful consideration of the delicate topic at hand. It is important to note the heavy cultural and religious sub text of this question too.

The scrutiny on women's body and choices have always been disproportionate and it is with the recent feminist movement that the idea of women's primary 'duty' being child birth is being shunned. This debate, while more controversial, is merely a repercussion of the broader movement for women's rights and liberties.

One of the easier to establish points is that abortion in the case of rape or incest or medical danger is simply unfair. It is unfair to demand a survivor of trauma or a potential survivor of trauma to carry through in a situation that is beyond their control. Such demands infringe on the rights of carrying mother leaving little room for justification of the fetus's rights. A pregnancy simply, cannot be continued in all circumstances and arguments against this fail to consider the position of the mother herself while defending a 'potential' human being.

The arguments against abortion seem to hold at heart the idea of a human being and consciousness in a moral sense rather than a biological sense. Yet it is this very idea that is hard to substantiate. The idea that a potential human being and a potential life should be given equal footing as an existing human being and life is hard to justify.

Further if this argument is valid in the case of abortion, it should also work in the case against birth control as that is also an act of denying potential life or human being. This points us to the root of the issue which is the case of reproductive rights and the fight to have control over it.

Living in a society where birth control (temporary) is still catered largely towards males and with a female population that lacks access to birth control methods or a basic understanding of the same it seems terribly unfair to deny abortions to the female group that bears a disproportionate impact of childbirth.

The umpteen number of back alley abortions, unsafe abortion methods and deaths during abortion point to the fact that at the end of the day abortion is not a matter of privilege for women but a necessity for their life.

The voices against abortions seem to argue a case for the universality of humanity yet at their core fail to uphold that very same value. Arguments centered around the many possibilities of life of an 'unborn' fetus hardly seem like a justification for the constraints and pressures it places on an ongoing life.

Further the more reasonable voices on the right which argue that instead of objection to person's desires they are simply arguing for a person's ideal desires. Yet this seems to propagates the same underlying belief that the ideal woman bears children, and desires motherhood, which appears to be stilted opinion rooted in our ideas of gender and religion.
