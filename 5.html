5: Is it ethical to patent or copyright genes, embryos or their parts?

The debates surrounding patents in medicine have been long debated regarding their merits and demerits. The inclusion of genes and embryos only makes this debate more complicated. Raw parts of nature are not patentable as they are seen as common or shared heritage. However, DNA when isolated, purified or modified to produce unique form which is no longer natural is patented.

A few issues regarding gene or embryo patenting is the person or group such patents help. If patented by a researcher or a company, royalties will have to payed to the owner of the patent. This could discourage others from pursuing further research in this area and could even be used as a means to suppress better methods or competition in upcoming fields.

Also, people may have to pay royalties to even look at a part of your genetic material or stifle invention when too many patents and hence, too many permissions and royalties payments need to be available.

However, in the absence of a patent system, there is no incentive for scientists to disclose their work. This could lead to the larger issue of isolated intellectuals repeating the same work or overlapping areas of research or disputes over intellectual properties. This could foster a culture of trade secrets or private pacts that are not beneficiary for scientific progress.

Further, a patent, unlike a property right, offers exclusive ownership only for a period of 20 years post which the restrictions are relaxed greatly. This provides the critical way for private research to find way quickly into common scientific knowledge.

This is not to say other factors such as profits, careers, fame and personal gain are not motivators behind filing for patents, particularly in a growing feed of gene biology.

However it is crucial to remember the fact that much of biomedical services and procedures available today have been ushered into existence through profit making machines. Tests, procedures, services and drugs do cost money and money is often the reason they are made available swiftly and widely. This is not to say the services made available justify money making but rather to substantiate that it is hard to separate the two motivations driving the boom in biomedical industry : for scientific discoveries and breakthroughs & higher profit margins.

The varying regulations of biomedical patents across the globe (EU vs USA) also point to a worrying development - the ethics of the medical discoveries we have made in the past few decades remain largely inconclusive.

The debate regarding gene patenting is part of the larger ongoing reckoning with commercialization of health care and related scientific technologies.

Monopolies are definitely a worrying phenomenon both morally and economically but, the patent rules and regulations do not conclusively help this. Privatization and capitalistic structures operate in the currency of money and profits in an industry that is ideally supposed to be run by compassion and altruism - creating a growing rift in the society.

Perhaps, the more worrying phenomenon at hand here is the exclusivity and the shake up of the growing biomedical industry whose ethical issues are yet to be evaluated in their entirety.
