9: Should We Prohibit the use of chimpanzees & other great apes in biomedical research?

The discussion regarding the usage of great apes, or chimpanzees primarily, is part of a broader discussion about animal use ethics.

It is taken as a common dictum that the more metal capacity an animal possesses the higher is its moral status. This is an anthropocentric view, since humans are the ones with most mental capacity, and is inevitably biased in our favor. But, it is not impossible to imagine that all these animals simply want to live without being molested or harmed or killed by humans for their 'higher' interests.

As Bertrand Russel said,

"There is no impersonal reason for regarding the interests of human beings as more important than that of animals".

However, we live a society that is structured around this human-centered view of the world - in our laws, institutions and company policies. The arguments that seek to justify this behavior inevitably revolve around the idea that animals simply do not suffer or 'feel' the way we do, and hence are less valuable.

However, the difference between chimpanzees and humans is less than the difference between gorillas and chimps as they share 94-98% of our DNA. Further, they have the capacity of self recognition in mirrors, rudimentary problem solving and a sense of justice and the future.

Chimps can self-actualize, in the sense, recognize themselves and view their own being through a deeper lens than most animals. Hence, they possess the capacity to 'feel' in the way we do - a fact as chimps in a laboratory if taught sign language can clearly ask their abuser to "stop", rather than through inarticulate protests. Groups from different geographical locations posses different cultures and social cues and are capable of planning for a 'future' and strategic thinking.

Often chimps have comparable IQs or metal capacities to those of children with Down's syndrome or humans with severe neurological conditions yet, chimps barely posses the same moral status as their human counterparts. This is not to argue that human rights must be extended to them, but rather that mental capacity is not a good criteria to evaluate the quality of life one is entitled to.

But, their similarity to us is what apparently makes them so valuable and their usage so extensive to biomedical research. However, this is not a universally accepted practice.

As the number of these endangered species in the wild drastically diminish, the numbers of chimps in biomedical research facilities is steadily growing. They are kept in cramped space and often in isolation and subjected to multiple procedures often at once, for blood sampling, biopsies, surgeries and injections of bacteria an viruses - many of which lead to death. Further the US National research council of institute of medicine has stated that " The present trajectory indicates a decreasing scientific need for chimpanzee studies due to the emergence of non-chimpanzee models and technologies". In light of the changing ideas around primates usage for research a number of countries have greatly limited or prohibited this practice.

In light of GAPA or the Great Apes Protection Act of 2009, which seeks to eliminative all invasive research on primates, the reasons behind this move should be re-examined.

(All primates already receive extra consideration under the federal Animal Welfare Act (originally passed by US Congress in 1966), which requires “a physical environment adequate to promote the psychological well-being of primates.”

Under the CHIMP act, passed in 2000, chimpanzees came to be favored in another way: if they survive research, and they are not useful for further research, they must be retired to a federally approved sanctuary, not killed as other animals would be)

The main argument against animal rights or GAPA act is the question that :

If a 1000 chimps lives are sacrificed for potentially saving millions of human lives is the trade off not worth it?

The argument that "since maximizing human lives and minimizing human suffering is our ultimate goal , every option that can get us there must be considered and the end results justify the process", is appealing. However it is violated nearly every minute on this planet.

Cigarettes, alcohol or obesity are not outlawed even if they could potentially save millions of human lives and greatly reduce suffering. Further, we do not spend every last bit of available resource to ensuring our prolongation. Art, music and nature must be cast aside as trivial pursuits in comparison to our grand goals of preservation, yet we recognize there is value beyond just survival.

Further, it is not such a simple equation of experiment and great results. Many chimps were used for HIV research, which has ultimately proven useless as they cannot develop AIDS like humans. There are also doubts regarding the usefulness of chimps in Hepatitis CC research as the disease spreads between people through blood and does not occur in chimp populations.

Since there is no 'greater' good achieved in this case, does their suffering just get cast aside as a side effect of scientific progress?

Additionally, what justifies the ratio of suffering to the results. In a scenario where torturing a person for a few days produces a vaccine against cancer should it be pursued with no ethical dilemma as it overall, does reduce human suffering? When chimps loose 50-60 years of their lives in labs, is it balanced by the 'potential' humans lives it could probably save?

The major reason for the rejection of GAPA is our speciesist thinking. When US doctors conducted experiments on prisoners or Nazis experimented on Non-Aryans why was there an outrage? These were people who believed that the suffering of a less-valuable few justified the benefits for the majority - the same thinking we still have around animal abuse. The justification for suffering cannot be an ignorant rhetoric of "because we are human and it helps us".

Oversight committees are not ethics committees and are merely directive bodies to enforce a set of regulations. The point is not to reduce suffering but reduce it to the level that is acceptable to us. Chimps must be researched on under anesthesia as we can provide for it. Cows can be tortured, overfed, branded and their horns broken off because that suffering allows for a profitable beef industry.

The world is clearly progressing towards greater inclusivity and advocation of animals' moral status. The protection of great apes- our closest relative genetically is merely the first step. Archaic arguments that justify suffering by saying it helps us or the majority are barely ethically justifiable and as attitudes change will loose the little footing that they stand on.
